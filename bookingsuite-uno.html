BookingSuite Uno

Background

Hospitality software is a terribly disjointed ecosystem. All but the smallest hotels manage up to 14 different vendor relationships. These commonly include:

  • Online travel agencies like Expedia and Booking.com
  • Property Management software, for running the day-to-day operations of the hotel
  • Channel Management software, for coordinating its rates across sites
  • Revenue Optimization software, for comp data on room pricing
  • Billing and invoicing software
  • Etc.

Mo' systems, mo' problems: syncs between the systems break, causing pricing and availability problems, paying for multiple systems is expensive, etc.

Having just been acquired by Booking.com, BookingSuite (FKA Buuteeq), was the former’s first foray into SaaS. Uno aimed to bring all of these disparate elements into one package. No player dominated this space, and if successful Uno could disrupt the entire industry.

My Role

When I started, the Lead PM and Design Director had largely defined the MVP feature specifications. My role was to tag-team along with the Design Director to flesh out the design and interaction, prototype and test these designs with hoteliers, and iterate and refine based on this feedback.

Quantitative Testing

Before investing too many resources in development, we wanted to validate our proposed core IA structure. I helped carry out a series of statistically significant reverse card-sort tests (tree tests) with over 10,000 hotel partners in Italy, Spain, the UK, and the US. A major outcome of the test was the finding that we had nested pages too many levels deep for users to comfortably navigate.

An in-depth analysis of the IA tree test

Qualitative Testing

I also carried out a series of eye-tracked, moderated usability sessions for our web and iOS apps. The goals of these sessions were to validate the problem space, gather general product/feature feedback, measure task completion, and detect general usability issues.

A Dramatic Pivot

As Uno was in pilot phase with 8 hotels, the project took a dramatic turn. Booking.com has a platform (the “Extranet”) on which over 20 million accommodation partners manage their room rates and availability. It didn’t make sense to start from square one with customer acquisition when we could have a massive and instantly addressable market if we integrated our product into the existing Extranet.

However, our tech stack did not play nicely with the Extranet. Nor could our designs simply be ported into this new environment; the Extranet has different design patterns and a different core IA. Most importantly, Booking.com practices a radically different design philosophy.

We needed to start again from scratch. To learn more about how I adapted to this new reality, and the organizational wins I was able to notch in my belt, check out the next project, Booking.com: Improving Host to Guest Communication.
