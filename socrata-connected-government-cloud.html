Socrata Connected Government CloudInternal Data-Sharing & Collaboration for Government

Background

This is a story about organizational change and how design can help shepherd an organization shifting its core business in a new direction.

Socrata Open Data

For over a decade, Socrata’s pioneering Open Data software has enabled government agencies to publish their data publicly, putting them in compliance with transparency laws, and fostering deeper citizen engagement. Agencies can take raw data from any system, and make it consumable in the form of engaging data “assets”–tabular datasets, data visualizations, published reports, etc.

Socrata brings government data to life in the form of engaging data "assets"

Socrata’s Second Act: The Innovator’s Dilemma

As a mature product with many newer entrants to the space, Socrata began to see the commoditization of its product. To survive, it had to jump the innovation “s-curve” and deliver new value to the market.

Through engagement with visionary customers, Socrata identified a much larger market opportunity; due to ubiquitous internal data silos, government workers often lack access to mission-critical data within their own agency. A data-starved agency is limited in its ability to make consensus- and data-driven policy decisions. Socrata aimed to address this gap by tuning its core public data-sharing platform to this internal data-sharing use case–Socrata Connected Government Cloud (SCGC).

My Role

A large ship steering in a new direction (especially one serving government customers) is a slow voyage with numerous squalls and unexpected icebergs. I joined Socrata when the vision for SCGC was being internalized by the wider org, and the product team was translating this vision into concrete requirements.

A basic outline of the MVP value SCGC would provide:

  • Collaboration: Often multiple subject-matter experts work together to produce a dataset or report.
  • Fine-grained access control: data might be sensitive. Users need control over which people/groups can access it, be it one person, a team, the entire agency, or the public
  • Authoritativeness: A clear demarcation is needed between data that is in progress—a sandbox for analysis—vs. data that is final and approved for wider dissemination.

Key Areas of Influence

I worked primarily on two components of SCGC. Each touched the entirety of the platform, and one I landed on entirely unexpectedly.

Challenge #1: The Publishing Model

SCGC required a far more complex model for creating and publishing data than Socrata’s existing Open Data platform. In the latter, small Open Data teams would collect datasets from within their agencies, inspect for accuracy, scrub PII, add necessary context, and then publish to the public.

SCGC, however, represented an entire ecosystem of data creation, internal collaboration, and sharing. Users needed highly granular access controls. Version control—who can see the working draft, and who can only see the finalized version—was also essential. Finally, departments are protective of their data and reputation, any form of data-sharing required an approval workflow.

Challenge #2: Unifying the UX

Parallel to my work on the publishing model, I encountered a problem that if left unaddressed would have strongly negative impacts on the overall UX of SCGC.

Users interact with Socrata primarily via the collection of data "assets" on Open Data sites: tables, visualizations, reports, maps, etc. Each are white-label experiences that embed within an agency’s site, having little information architecture and navigational structure of their own.

In SCGC, we were attempting to build a cohesive app, where users would be enticed to do the bulk of their daily analytical and collaborative work. But there was little consistency in the way users would take critical actions upon each asset, like publishing and other CRUD operations.

Imagine A Google Drive experience where sharing, inviting to collaborate, editing looked completely different in Docs vs. Sheets vs. Slides. Would this friction make users more or less likely to use Google Drive as their core productivity and collaboration suite?

I felt strongly that our MVP would see its impact muffled without implementing some level of UX consistency. Advocating for this work while the project was in-flight and during this tumultuous time for the organization was (and remains) one of the most challenging experience of my career. The task ahead was to:

  • Socialize my view within the wider product team
  • Carve out time to research and design MVP-deliverable solutions
  • Work with Engineering Managers and PMs to understand technical limitations and phase development
  • Adequately communicate and assuage stakeholders who felt left out of the decision-making process

I found the best consensus-building weapon in my arsenal was to proactively and transparently share the findings from my research and usability testing.

Ironically, the result of all this was a rather small UI element. To unify each of the wildly different asset types, and to do so without a complete overhaul, required being as unobtrusive as possible. I designed an “actions bar” that floated at the top of the screen and provided a consistent home and jumping-off point for the collaboration and publishing features of SCGC.

Results

A mere month before MVP launch, Socrata was acquired by the Fortune 100 company Tyler Technologies. I learned that my designs were shown to the highest echelons of Tyler, and it was in part the promise of a seamless and consistent internal data-sharing experience that made Tyler see Socrata as an acquisition that dovetails with their major investment in their Connected Communities line of business.

Today, SCGC is meaningfully increasing Average Recurring Revenue across Tyler's customers and is helping various business units expand aggressively. The acquisition has so far proven to be highly beneficial for Tyler Tech. The publishing model and unifying UX I worked on are now the standard platform experience.
