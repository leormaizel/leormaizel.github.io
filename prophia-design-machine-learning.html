Prophia Designing for Scale with Machine Learning

Background

For the Commercial Real Estate (CRE) firm, the most critical source of business data is the lease document; it drives budget projections, buying/selling strategies, etc. Unfortunately, leases tend to be physical documents locked in a fireproof cabinet, or PDFs lost in a jumbled share drive, leading to several downstream problems:

  • Lease administrators must refer to the lease document to answer all but the simplest questions.
  • Key lease terms must be manually entered into accounting systems, budgeting, etc.
  • The more removed data gets from the source document the higher the likelihood of discrepancies between systems.

Prophia solves these problems by extracting critical data straight from the source document, and presenting it in a standardized, dynamic, and extensible manner. And it does so at scale.

Prophia connects lease data and everything that is built on top of it back to the original document

But how did we get here?

Phase 1: Establishing a baseline manual process

Given limited MVP resources, we knew we'd have to start with a manual process for capturing the relevant data in each document. I designed an experience whereby our in-house team of “onboarders” could view the document, “tag” the critical data, and transform it into a standardized format.

When we released this functionality with the Prophia MVP, we achieved a critical business milestone— we had the functionality needed to onboard customer data. But it was painfully manual, as each set of lease documents took about 3.5 hours to onboard.

Phase 2: Scaling the process w/ Machine Learning

As we signed more beta customers, we approached a bottleneck in onboarding capabilities. As we hired more onboarders, we experimented with Machine Learning-based methods for interpreting and capturing textual information.

Based on the promising initial results of these experiments, the product team collaboratively devised a 3-stage approach to operationalizing this technology:

  • Stage 1: The model lacks sufficient training data to make accurate text predictions. However, it can alert the onboarder that a portion of text appears “significant”.
  • Stage 2: Once we are sufficiently confident in the model's recall and accuracy, predict which data points are likely found within the text and present these predictions to the onboarder for verification.
  • Stage 3: Automatically capture data points where the model has demonstrated its predictive abilities with sufficient accuracy

I experimented with several concepts for how best to indicate where significant portions of text lie, and what they might contain:

After prototyping and testing these concepts with onboarders, findability was a clear area of improvement. Many leases are hundreds of pages long; suggestions are useless unless the onboarder knows that they exist and where to find them. My solution involved a suggestions table of contents, and a custom scrollbar indicating a suggestion's location.

An all-together view of the ML-powered features, including suggestions in the document, a suggestions ToC, and a custom scrollbar with suggestion indicators

Measuring Results

Measuring the performance of these features required overcoming several obstacles:

  • Each new-hire onboarder experiences a learning curve in interpreting lease documents
  • The onboarding team continuously changes/improves their processes
  • We regularly add to the number of data points we capture

Despite these difficulties, our ML-aided data capture process has demonstrated clear value:

  • Half of all data points are captured 100% automatically, freeing our onboarders to focus on more complex lease terms
  • The text suggestion features I designed have decreased onboarding time by an average of 64%, from 3.5 to 1.25 hours per building.

This ML-powered ingress, extraction, and interpretation pipeline is in no small part responsible for our success in expanding with existing customers, handling large expansions in a timely manner, and securing VC investment.
