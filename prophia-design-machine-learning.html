Prophia Designing for Scale with Machine Learning

Background

For the Commercial Real Estate (CRE) firm, there is no source of business data more critical than the lease document; it drives cash flows, budget projections, buying/selling strategies, etc. Unfortunately, this data suffers from a debilitating flaw—it is always in the form of a physical document locked in a fireproof cabinet, or a PDF lost in a jumbled share drive, leading to several downstream problems:

  • Non-scalability: lease administrators must manually refer to the document to answer all but the simplest questions.
  • Organizational inefficiency: since this data isn’t extensible, it must be manually entered into all other 3rd-party systems (i.e. accounting, budgeting, etc.).
  • Data discrepancies: the more removed data gets from the source document the higher the likelihood of error.

The Prophia platform solves these problems by extracting critical lease data straight from the source document, presenting it in a standardized, dynamic, and extensible manner, and it does so at scale.

Prophia connects lease data and everything that is built on top of it back to the original document

But how did we get here?

Phase 1: Establishing a baseline manual process

For MVP, we knew we'd need to start off with a manual process; I needed to build an experience whereby our in-house team of “onboarders” could read the lease document, “tag” the critical data, categorize it, and transform it into the required format.

Phase 2: Scaling the process w/ Machine Learning

As we signed up more beta customers, no path to scalability was possible without leveraging Machine Learning to interpret and extract the data. The whole product team worked collaboratively to assess the feasibility of various solutions, and how to operationalize them in the UX. Our approach was divided into 3 stages:

  • Stage 1: The model lacks sufficient training data to make accurate predictions of the meaning of the text. However, it is capable of alerting the onboarder that a portion of text appears “significant”.
  • Stage 2: Once we are sufficiently confident in the training data, build a model that predicts which data points are likely to be found within a significant portion of text. Surface these predictions to the onboarder for human verification.
  • Stage 3: Automatically capture data points where the model has demonstrated its predictive abilities with sufficient accuracy

I focused my efforts on stages 1 & 2 since we had greater confidence in our ability to deliver on them. I experimented with several concepts for how best to indicate where significant portions of text lie, and what they might contain:

After prototyping and testing these concepts with our onboarders, a clear area of improvement was findability; many leases are hundreds of pages long and suggestions are useless unless the onboarder knows that they exist and where to find them. My solution involved a suggestions table of contents, and a custom scrollbar indicating the locations of each.

Measuring Results

As it turns out, measuring the performance of these features proved devilishly hard, because:

  • We’re constantly hiring new onboarders, each of which experiences a learning curve with interpreting lease documents
  • The onboarding team is continuously updating and improving their processes
  • We regularly add to the number of data points we capture
  • Etc.

Despite this difficulty, a clear demonstration of value is the fact that we are now capturing more data points via ML than have ever been captured by human onboarders. This ML-powered ingress, extraction, and interpretation pipeline is in no small part responsible for our success in expanding with existing customers, signing up new customers, and securing VC investment.
