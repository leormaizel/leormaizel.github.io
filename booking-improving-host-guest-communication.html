Booking.comImproving Host to Guest Communication

Background

As described in the previous project, BookingSuite Uno, the MVP of BookingSuite’s all-in-one hotel management product took a dramatic turn just as it had entered pilot. Booking.com (the parent company of BookingSuite) already had a platform (the “Extranet”) on which 20 million+ accommodation partners manage their rates and availability. It didn’t make sense to release the product on a new platform with only handful of users, when we could have a massive and instantly addressable market if we integrated into the Extranet.

However, our tech stack did not play nicely with the Extranet and our designs could not simply be ported over. We had to re-design and re-build Uno from the ground up.

Adapting to a New World

The Extranet had vastly different design patterns and core IA. Most importantly, Booking.com practices a radically different design philosophy; whereas the team at BookingSuite was accustomed to large-scale greenfield products, Booking.com is organizationally committed to small-scale A/B testing, where the all changes must be defended with statistical significance.

I needed to redesign Uno in a phased approach; introduce the least amount of change, A/B test, and then proceed to the next feature.

A screen of the Booking.com hotelier extranet "Today" page. With a vastly different IA and feature set, redesigning Uno would be quite the challenge.

Redesigning the Dashboard: Research/Design Process

One of the goals of Uno was to enable the hotelier to see the most critical information about their hotel in a central, upfront location. This included:

  • Messages from guests
  • Estimated guest arrival times
  • Check-ins/outs for the day
  • Reservation notifications

The Extranet’s hotelier dashboard lacked much of this functionality. But before I added features to the dashboard, I wanted to gain a baseline of what was and wasn’t working well.

I conducted remote usability tests with 7 different hotel partners in Europe, North, South, and Central America and was able to draw upon reams of research generated by Booking.com’s in-house researchers.

The data we gathered for this page was not encouraging:

  • The existing dashboard was far too information-dense; it showed guest arrivals and departures, recent Booking.com reviews, new bookings, and guest messages jumbled together with no discernible content hierarchy or separation
  • As a result, users were not reliably able to find any specific piece of information, such as when a guest might be arriving. One such instance was relayed with great embarrassment by an Amsterdam hotelier: “it wasn’t just ‘Hi, can I have another pillow?’, it was ‘I’m getting to the airport at 3 am, how can I get to your hotel?’ The guest was abandoned.”
  • Virtually nothing on the page was actionable without having to navigate elsewhere

Taking this feedback into account, I designed a version of the dashboard that organized the data by category and provided immediate next steps for each item.

Evaluation Process: A/B Testing

Normally, I'd want to prototype and test my designs for usability before development. However, the culture at Booking.com heavily favored A/B testing over more qualitative methods. As a new member of the Booking.com org, I didn’t feel I had the organizational clout to push the envelope quite yet.

The results of the test were overwhelmingly positive. My design increased next-step actions, such as hosts responding to guest messages by 431%. The redesigned dashboard decreased inbound support requests, saving more than 700 customer support hours per quarter.

Conclusion

During my time at Booking.com, the experiment was turned “full-on” and released to approximately 1 million hotel partners around the world.

Designers from the HQ office in Amsterdam took notice and even reached out to learn about our product design philosophy and qualitative feedback-gathering methods. I’d like to think I’ve made a small dent in their universe.
