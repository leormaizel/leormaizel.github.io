Booking.comImproving Host to Guest Communication

Background

As described in the previous project, BookingSuite Uno, the MVP of BookingSuite’s all-in-one hotel management product took a dramatic turn just as it had entered pilot. Booking.com (the parent company of BookingSuite) already had a platform (the “Extranet”) on which over 20 million accommodation partners manage their room rates and availability. It didn’t make sense to start from square one with customer acquisition on our own platform when we could have a massive and instantly addressable market if we integrated into the existing Extranet.

However, our tech stack did not play nicely with the Extranet and our designs could not simply be ported over. We had to design and build anew.

Adapting to a New World

the Extranet had different design patterns and core IA. Most importantly, Booking.com practices a radically different design philosophy; whereas the team at BookingSuite was accustomed to large-scale green field products, Booking.com is organizationally devoted to small-scale A/B testing, where the most minute change must be defended with statistical significance. I needed to redesign of Uno in a phased approach; I’d design for the least amount of change, A/B test, and then proceed to the next feature.

A screen of the Booking.com hotelier extranet "Today" page. With a vastly different IA and featureset, redesigning Uno would be quite the challenge.

Redesigning the Dashboard: Research/Design Process

One of the goals of Uno was to enable the hotelier to see the most critical information about their hotel in a central, upfront location. This included:

  • Messages from guests
  • Estimated guest arrival times
  • Check-ins/outs for the day
  • Reservation notifications
  • Etc.

The Extranet’s hotelier dashboard had some of this functionality but lacked others critical to Uno’s value proposition. Before I added features to the dashboard, I wanted to gain a baseline of what was and wasn’t working well.

I conducted remote usability tests with 14 different hotel partners in Europe, North, South, and Central America and was able to draw upon reams of research generated by Booking.com’s in-house researchers.

The data we gathered for this page was not encouraging:

  • CSAT numbers for the Extranet were extremely poor
  • The existing dashboard was far too information-dense; it showed guest arrivals and departures, recent Booking.com reviews, new bookings, and messages sent from the guest all together with no discernable content hierarchy or separation
  • As a result, users were not reliably able to find any specific piece of information, such as when a guest might be arriving today. This had disastrous outcomes such as a hoteliers missing a critical guest message. One such instance was relayed with great embarrassment by the owner of a hotel in Amsterdam: “it wasn’t just ‘Hi, can I have another pillow?’, it is things like ‘I’m getting to the airport at 3 am, how can I get to your hotel?’ The guest felt abandoned.”
  • Virtually nothing on the page was actionable without having to navigate elsewhere

Taking this feedback into account, I designed a version of the dashboard that organized the data by category and provided immediate next steps for each item. For some items, like arrivals and departures, chronology was of paramount importance; the hotelier needed to know which guests were arriving/departing first. For other items, like new guest reviews, chronology was trivial—it mattered less when the event occurred than that it occurred.

Evaluation Process: A/B Testing

Normally, I'd want to prototype and test my designs for usability. However, the culture at Booking.com heavily favored A/B testing over more qualitative methods. As a new member of the Booking.com org, I didn’t feel I had the organizational clout to push the envelope quite yet.

The results of the test were overwhelmingly positive. My design increased next-step actions, such as hosts responding to guest messages by 391%. By this metric, this experiment was in the 97th percentile of all experiments tracked at Booking.com (keeping in mind that my changes were much larger in scope than normal). The redesigned dashboard decreased inbound support requests, saving more than 700 customer support hours per quarter.

Conclusion

During my time at Booking.com, the experiment was turned “full-on” and released to approximately 1 million hotel partners around the world.

Designers from the HQ office in Amsterdam took notice and even reached out to learn about our product design philosophy and qualitative feedback-gathering methods. I’d like to think I’ve made a small dent in their universe.
